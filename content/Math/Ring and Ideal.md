A ring is an abelian group $R$ with a multiplication operation $(a,b) \rightarrow ab$ and an identity element $1$, satisfying for all $a,b,c \in R$: 
$$\begin{array}{l} a(bc)=b(ac) \\ a(b+c) = ab+ac \\ (b+c)a = ba+ca \\ 1a = a1 = a \end{array}$$
A ring $R$ is commutative if, in addition, $ab = ba$ for all $a,b \in R$. 
A unit or invertible element in a ring $R$ is an element $u$ such that there is an element $v\in R$ such that $uv = 1$. Such a $v$ is unique and it is denoted as $u^{-1}$. 
A field is a ring in which every nonzero element is invertible. 
Next, we have an ideal in a commutative ring $R$ is an additive subgroup $I$ such that for every $r\in R$ and $s \in I$ then $rs \in I$. Also for each $f,g \in I$ then $f+g\in I$ (since $I$ is an additive subgroup). 
An Ideal $I$ is said to be generated by a subset $S \subset R$ if every element $t \in I$ can be written in the form 
$$ t = \sum_{i=1}^n r_is_i$$
with $r_i$ in $R$ and $s_i \in S$ and we denote this ideal by $(S)$. An ideal is principal if it can be generated by one element
$$ I = (a) = \lbrace ra \ | \ r \in R\rbrace$$
An ideal of $I$ of a commutative ring $R$ is prime if $I \neq R$ and if $f,g \in R$ and $fg \in I$ implies $f \in I$ or $g \in 1$. 
Let’s not forget that we are studying algebraic geometry, where our main goal is to solve some systems of polynomial equations. Ideals provide an abstract way to represent sets of polynomials, and by using Gröbner bases we can transform these systems into multivariate polynomials with desirable algorithmic properties.
