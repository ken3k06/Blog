# Introduction to Ideal


A ring is an abelian group $R$ with a multiplication operation $(a,b) \rightarrow ab$ and an identity element $1$, satisfying for all $a,b,c \in R$: 
$$
\begin{array}{l} a(bc)=b(ac) \\ a(b+c) = ab+ac \\ (b+c)a = ba+ca \\ 1a = a1 = a \end{array}
$$

A ring $R$ is commutative if, in addition, $ab = ba$ for all $a,b \in R$. 
A unit or invertible element in a ring $R$ is an element $u$ such that there is an element $v\in R$ such that $uv = 1$. Such a $v$ is unique and it is denoted as $u^{-1}$. 
A field is a ring in which every nonzero element is invertible. 
Next, we have an ideal in a commutative ring $R$ is an additive subgroup $I$ such that for every $r\in R$ and $s \in I$ then $rs \in I$. Also for each $f,g \in I$ then $f+g\in I$ (since $I$ is an additive subgroup). 
An Ideal $I$ is said to be generated by a subset $S \subset R$ if every element $t \in I$ can be written in the form 

$$ 
t = \sum_{i=1}^n r_is_i
$$

with $r_i$ in $R$ and $s_i \in S$ and we denote this ideal by $(S)$. An ideal is principal if it can be generated by one element

$$
I = (a) = \lbrace ra \ | \ r \in R\rbrace
$$

An ideal of $I$ of a commutative ring $R$ is prime if $I \neq R$ and if $f,g \in R$ and $fg \in I$ implies $f \in I$ or $g \in 1$.

Ideals provide an abstract way to represent sets of polynomials, and by using GrĂ¶bner bases we can transform these systems into multivariate polynomials with desirable algorithmic properties.

# Affine varieties
Let $k$ be a field and let $k^n$ denote the set of all $n$ - tuples:
$$ 
k^n = \lbrace a=(a_1,...,a_n) : a_i \in k \ \text{for all} \ i \rbrace
$$
We use the abbreviation 
$$ 
X = (x_1,...,x_n)
$$
so that we can conveniently denote the polynomial ring $k[x_1,...,x_n]$ by $k[X]$. A polynomial $f(X) \in k[X]$ determine polynomial function $k^n \rightarrow k$. 
Now let assume that we are working on infinite fields. 
**Definition 1.** If $f(X) \in k[X]$ and $f(a) = 0$ where $a \in k^n$ then $a$ is called a zero of $f(X)$. If $f(x)$ is an univariate polynomial then $x$ is called a root of $f$. 
**Proposition 1.** If $k$ is an algebraically closed field and $f(X) \in k[X]$ is not a constant then $f(X)$ has a zero.
(The proof is left as an exercise for Math student <("))
**Definition 2.** If $F$ is a subset of $k[X]$ then the affine variety defined by $F$ is 
$$ 
V(F) = \lbrace a \in k^n : f(a) = 0, \forall f(X) \in F \rbrace; 
$$
which mean $V(F)$ consists of all those $a \in k^n$ which are zeros of every $f(X) \in F$. 
**Proposition 2.** Let $k$ be a field and let $F,G$ be subsets of $k[X]$. 
i/ If $F \subseteq G \subseteq k[X]$ then $V(G)\subseteq V(F)$ 
ii/ If $F \subseteq k[X]$ and $I = (F)$ is the ideal generated by $F$, then 
$$ 
V(F) = V(I)
$$

Let $I$ and $J$ be two ideals of $k[X]$. An ideal generated by $I \cup J$ is called the sum of $I$ and $J$, we denote this by $I + J$. An ideal generated by the product of $fg$ for each $f\in I$ and $g\in J$ is called the product ideal of $I$ and $J$ and denote by $IJ$. 

Thus, 
$$ 
\begin{array}{l} I + J = \lbrace f+g \ | f \in I, g \in J \rbrace  \\ IJ = \lbrace f_1g_1+...f_rg_r \ | f_i \in I,g_i\in J , r \geq1 \rbrace\end{array}
$$

# Monomials Orders
Given two polynomials $f(x),g(x) \in k[x]$ with $g(x) \neq 0$, where $k$ is a field. The Division Algorithm gives unique polynomials $q(x),r(x) \in k[x]$ with 
$$
f(x) = q(x)g(x)+r(x)
$$

where $r=0$ or $\deg(r) < \deg(g)$ and $g | f$ if and only if the remainder $r=0$. 
Now let us look at this formula from a different point of view. To say that $g | f$ is to say that $f \in (g)$, the principal ideal generated by $g(x)$. Thus, the remainder $r$ is the obstruction to $f$ lying in this ideal, that is $f \in (g)$ if and only if $r = 0$. 
Now let extend this: Given polynomials 

$$ 
f(x), g_1(x),...,g_m(x) \in k[x]
$$


where $k$ is a field, when is $f \in I = (g_1,...,g_m)$.?

We now ask whether there is an algorithm in $k[x_1,...,x_n]=k[X]$ to determine, given $f(X),g_1(X),...,g_m(X)\in k[X]$, whether $f \in (g_1,...,g_m)$. A generalized Division algorithm in $k[X]$ should be an algorithm yielding

$$ 
r(X),a_1(X),...,a_m(X) \in k[X]
$$

with $r(X)$ unique such that:

$$ 
f= a_1g_1+...+a_mg_m + r
$$

**Definition 1.** The degree of a nonzero monomial $cx_1^{a_1}...x_n^{a_n} = cX^a \in k[X]$ is the $n$-tuple $a = (a_1,...,a_n) \in \mathbb{N}^n$. We write 
$$ 
\deg(cX^a) = a
$$
The weight $|a|$ of $cX^a$ is the sum $|a| = a_1+...+a_n \in \mathbb{N}$
Now let $\Omega$ be a well-ordered set. [Wiki](https://en-m-wikipedia-org.translate.goog/wiki/Well-order?_x_tr_sl=en&_x_tr_tl=vi&_x_tr_hl=vi&_x_tr_pto=tc) Then:
i/ $\Omega$ is a chain, that is, if $x,y \in \Omega$ then either $x \prec y$ or $y\prec x$. 
ii/ Every strictly decreasing sequence in $\Omega$ is finite.
We will use the second property of well-ordered sets to show that the algorithm will eventually stops. Since the set $\mathbb{N}$ of natural numbers, equipped with the usual inequality $\leq$ is well-ordered, any strictly decreasing sequence of natural numbers must be finite. So if the algorithm yielding the $\gcd$ does not stop at a given step, the then the the degree of an associated polynomial to the next step is strictly smaller which is a contradiction. 
**Definition 2.** A monomial order is a well ordering of $\mathbb{N}^n$ such that 
$$ 
\alpha \preceq \beta \Rightarrow \alpha + \gamma \preceq \beta + \gamma
$$
for all $\alpha,\beta,\gamma \in \mathbb{N}^n$
A monomial order of $\mathbb{N}^n$ gives a well-ordering of monomials in $k[X]$: Let 
$$ 
X^\alpha \preceq X^\beta
$$

if $\alpha \preceq \beta$. Thus monomials are ordered according to their degrees.
